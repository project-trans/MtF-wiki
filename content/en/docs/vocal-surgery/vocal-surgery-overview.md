{{< notice warning >}}

Because laryngeal intubation under general anesthesia may irritate the vocal cords, surgery under general anesthesia is generally not recommended within one year after vocal cord surgery. If you plan to have sex reassignment surgery, tell your doctor proactively; they may recommend doing it under spinal anesthesia.

{{< /notice >}}

{{< notice warning >}}

Outcomes of vocal cord surgery vary by person and may involve serious side effects. Possible side effects include reduced volume, severe hoarseness, and suture disruption from severe coughing after surgery. Results may range from satisfactory to minimal change and cannot be predicted. Consider this carefully before deciding on surgery.

{{< /notice >}}

## Overview {#overview}

Before voice feminization surgery, the following are commonly required:

1. Four {{< wiki infectious-disease >}} tests
1. Voice examination
   Items may include high/low pitch, vocal range, phonation pattern, maximum phonation time, fundamental frequency, and so on.
1. [Laryngoscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laryngoscopy)

In principle, [voice feminization training]({{< ref "vocal-therapy-overview" >}}) is recommended first. Voice training can change the way you phonate, word choice, and other aspects that are not determined by fundamental frequency alone.

Voice feminization surgery can only change vocal frequency, and loudness will be reduced to varying degrees.

If you are not sure why your voice does not meet expectations, you can measure your fundamental frequency yourself or receive a voice assessment at a hospital that provides voice training.

Some daily habits also affect the voice. The following usually cause consequences only when long-term or excessive:

- Smoking, including e-cigarettes and secondhand smoke
  - Smoking can cause vocal cord swelling and significantly affect the voice.
- Alcohol
- Coffee
- Staying up late
- Eating food or drinking large amounts of water within 2-3 hours before sleep
  - This may cause acid reflux and irritate the vocal cords.
- Irritating foods
  - Chili peppers, acidic foods, ice water, and similar items.

{{< notice info >}}

Surgery is not a cure-all: voice training is still needed after surgery to adjust articulation, vocal presentation, and related aspects.

{{< /notice >}}

In hospitals in mainland China, endoscopic [vocal fold shortening]({{< ref "#method" >}}) is currently the main procedure. Cricothyroid approximation is mainly used when vocal fold shortening is unsuitable, or when [tracheal shave]({{< ref "tracheal-shave" >}}) is performed at the same time and cricothyroid approximation is requested.

Generally, irritating foods should be avoided for three months after surgery (follow your doctor's instructions). Coughing can cause postoperative suture disruption. Some hospitals therefore add medication at the surgical site during surgery to help prevent coughing and speaking.

Postoperative {{< wiki hoarseness >}} or pitch lowering is a relatively serious postoperative complication.

## Requirements {#prerequirements}

> Requirements vary by hospital. Overall, prerequisites for voice surgery in mainland China are much looser than for SRS, but the specific requirements depend on the consultation.

## Factors Affecting Voice {#key-factor}

Speech fundamental frequency, resonance, intonation, voice quality, clarity, speaking intensity, speech rate, and prosody.

### Situations That May Be Unsuitable for Vocal Cord Surgery {#not-suitable}

{{< notice warning "Note" >}}
The following situations come from cases where doctors stated during consultation that surgery was unsuitable. In practice, the final decision still depends on both the doctor and the patient.
{{< /notice >}}

- Congenital vocal cord dysplasia
- Incomplete vocal fold closure
- Glottic insufficiency
- Excessive vocal fold laxity
- Overly limited phonation pattern
