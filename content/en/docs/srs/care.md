## Preoperative Supplies

This list does not include items the hospital provides, such as dilators. Some supplies and medicines listed below may also be provided by the institution.

### Clothing

1. Nightgown, loose dress-like sleepwear, or other loose clothing
1. Slippers or shoes that are easy to put on without bending

### Disposable Care Supplies

{{< notice info "About pads" >}}
Many pads list only length, not width. Pads that are too wide may press on the vulva and cause discomfort.

In the early postoperative period, avoid pads with cooling ingredients or fragrance if possible. Menthol-like ingredients may sting, and fragrance can add risk.

Avoid liners that are too small, as they can shift and cause discomfort. People prone to allergies can consider reusable organic cotton pads.
{{< /notice >}}

{{< notice info "About lubricant" >}}
Choose lubricant according to the surgeon's instructions. In general, water-based products with some viscosity may be easier to use for dilation.
{{< /notice >}}

1. Waterproof mattress cover or nursing pads for wound leakage and routine dilation
1. Daytime pads
1. Night pads
1. Gauze rolls, non-adherent gauze pads, medical tape, paper tape, cloth tape, or hypoallergenic tape
1. Lubricant. If cleaning the dilator is difficult, ask the doctor whether using a condom over it is acceptable

### Medicines

Warning: medicines carry risks. Consult a doctor before use.

Common medicines:

1. Painkillers
   - Acetaminophen or ibuprofen may be useful
   - Opioids such as codeine or tramadol require medical supervision
1. Anti-inflammatory or antibiotic medicines
1. Lidocaine gel or other topical anesthetics, sometimes used for early dilation

Medicines sometimes used for wound care:

1. Antibacterial ointments or gels
1. Topical clindamycin or similar medicines
1. Erythromycin-type ointments
1. Metronidazole vaginal gel or similar medicines for inflammation or granulation tissue

Appearance and scar care:

1. **Scar care gel** and sheets, including polyurethane sheets. These may cause inflammation, so ask the doctor first
1. Topical medicines for pigmentation or raised scars

Other useful medicines and supplies:

1. Topical steroids for redness or allergy caused by long-term tape use
1. Antihistamines for itching or hives
1. Fiber products or short-term laxatives for constipation caused by painkillers
1. Cough syrup, lozenges, ginger tea, or similar items for sore throat after intubation
1. Antiemetics for nausea
1. Enemas or similar medicines, because anesthesia and painkillers can cause constipation

### Convenience Supplies

1. Thick bendable straw if liquid food is needed
1. Bendable straw or cup with straw for drinking while in bed
1. **Bed tray** for keeping common supplies within reach
1. **Donut cushion**, pregnancy pillow, or other pillows for support
1. Document folder for medical certificates, receipts, travel, or legal paperwork

### Entertainment and Work

1. Prepare videos, books, or other low-effort entertainment for long periods in bed
1. A laptop is easier to use with a bed tray
1. If you cannot sit up yet, a phone may be easier than a tablet or handheld console

## Postoperative Diet

Diet often progresses from liquids to soft food and then normal food. After discharge, eat enough protein and nutrients to support wound healing and recovery. Some fasting rules exist to reduce contamination of the surgical area by excretion.

In the early postoperative period, avoid excessive calories and sugar. Prefer low-sodium, minimally processed foods, fruit, vegetables, and adequate protein. Fried, heavily roasted, smoked, puffed snack foods, and spicy foods may be restricted by the doctor.

## Activity

Postoperative exercise restrictions aim to prevent wound separation and infection.

For a period after surgery, bed rest and avoidance of exercise are required. Duration depends on the surgeon, technique, and nursing conditions. Change position as allowed to reduce pressure sores.

After long bed rest is no longer needed, avoid strenuous exercise until cleared by the doctor. Avoid activities that acutely stretch or press the genital area, such as yoga, aerobics, Pilates, swimming, squats, heavy lifting, cycling, horseback riding, and similar activities.

Short walks may be recommended to support recovery.

## Sitting

Sitting can be uncomfortable during the first month, but is not necessarily unsafe. A donut cushion can reduce pressure on the surgical site. Do not use a U-shaped neck pillow as a substitute without medical approval, because sitting off-center may press on the wound.

## Travel

Even if someone can walk for a long time at discharge, using a wheelchair or mobility support is safer when travel is necessary. For flights or trains, consult the hospital and transport provider in advance and book assistance services.

Changing identity documents shortly before travel can cause problems with booked transportation or border procedures. Plan carefully if travel is expected.

## Hygiene and Vaginal Discharge {#hygiene-discharge}

Hygiene is especially important after surgery. Good hygiene helps prevent infection and can reduce odor and discomfort, improving mental well-being.

### Bathing and Showering

During hospitalization or stays in designated accommodation, nursing services may include sponge bathing.

With medical permission, showering may be allowed, meaning washing without soaking the lower body. Water contact may be allowed after vulvar wounds have healed. Wash the surgical area especially before and after dilation, and do not wear a dilator while bathing.

Baths, prolonged soaking, and bath additives require caution. Most institutions advise against bathing soon after surgery. Even if the wound looks healed, visible absorbable sutures can soften in warm water and tear more easily.

Useful items include dry shampoo, wet wipes, body wipes, antibacterial soap, hand sanitizer, shower hose, and long-handled sponge.

Avoid swimming in the early postoperative period.

### Toileting and Cleaning the Surgical Area

The vagina and vulva require special cleaning and, when indicated, antibacterial ointment. Wipe from front to back after toileting. This is important not only after surgery but also because of female anatomy.

After urination or bowel movements, clean the perineum carefully. Use a shower head, bidet, or wet wipes and wipe **front to back** to avoid bringing feces into the vagina. Wash hands before cleaning the genitals, then pat dry with a wet wipe or soft towel.

After basic healing, immediate washing after every toilet visit may no longer be needed. However, after urination, blot dry instead of rubbing hard.

During the early period of frequent dilation, the vulva may need daily washing. After dilation frequency decreases, washing may decrease to two or three times weekly. Vaginal douching should only be done if the doctor allows it.

The urinary catheter is usually removed after several days. Keep the urine bag below waist level and check that the tube is not kinked. If fluid intake is adequate but urine does not drain, tell a nurse or doctor.

Tell medical staff promptly during hospitalization if you have:

1. No urine flow despite adequate fluid intake
1. Large amounts of blood or clots in urine
1. Severe abdominal cramps
1. Signs of urinary tract infection, such as cloudy urine, fever, or pain

After catheter removal, urination and holding urine require adjustment. Relaxation, deep breathing, and gentle abdominal pressure may help. Because the urethra is shorter, the ability to hold urine may be lower than before surgery. Carrying spare underwear can be useful.

### Hair

Electrolysis in the perineal area may be discouraged for about a year after surgery. Some hair may grow inside the vagina after surgery.

### Vaginal Discharge

In flap-based techniques, the vagina is lined with skin such as foreskin or scrotal skin. Skin flora and some vaginal flora can grow there. Sweat glands and skin turnover can leave sebum, keratin, dead skin, lubricant, and semen residue. These materials may ferment and cause yellowish staining or odor.<sup>(UCSF care guide)</sup>

In sigmoid colon or other intestinal techniques, the vagina is lined with bowel tissue, so intestinal secretions and intestinal odor may occur.

In peritoneal techniques, clear discharge may be relatively abundant.

Discharge containing harmful microbes can enter the urethra and cause urinary tract infection.

When odor or discharge occurs:

1. Check whether the odor or discharge exceeds a normal range
1. Check for internal infection, abscess, lesions, or granulation tissue, and seek medical care if present
1. Check for small internal wounds such as granulation tissue or vaginal wall tears. Occasional bleeding can cause odor or infection if not cleaned
1. In flap-based techniques, odor may vary with hormone levels, but evidence is limited
1. If persistent odor or discharge is not due to infection, lesions, granulation tissue, or bleeding, remove thick discharge with a cotton swab and wash the perineum with clean water. If flora imbalance is suspected, consult a doctor

Pads can be used to prevent short-term bleeding and discharge from staining underwear. Choose thin, good-quality, fragrance-free products when possible.

## Sex and Sensation

Sexual activity should prioritize comfort and safety.

After surgery, sexual pleasure and orgasm may come from stimulation of the retained prostate, clitoral tissue, or nerve tissue placed inside the vagina in some techniques. After adequate healing and when safe, people may try vaginal penetration, clitoral friction, masturbation, or other activities. Postoperative pleasure can help integrate the vulva as part of one's body and support self-acceptance.

Sexual arousal disorder, reduced sexual interest, orgasm difficulties, painful intercourse, and vaginismus occur in cisgender women and may also occur in postoperative trans women. If these occur, relax, learn from other women's experiences, and use sex toys or other aids with pleasure and safety as the goal.

Further reading:

- Healing Painful Sex: A Woman's Guide to Confronting, Diagnosing, and Treating Sexual Pain, Deborah Coady, MD and Nancy Fish, MSW, MPH
- The Joy of Sex, Alex Comfort
- Becoming Orgasmic: A Sexual and Personal Growth Program for Women, Julia Heiman and Joseph Lopiccolo
- For Women Only: A Revolutionary Guide to Overcoming Sexual Dysfunction and Reclaiming Your Sex Life, Jennifer Berman, Laura Berman and Elisabeth Bumiller

For about three months, sexual arousal may be discouraged because genital congestion can cause pain, swelling, or delayed healing.

Vaginal penetrative sex may be completely avoided for six months or as directed. Starting between six months and one year, after vaginal scars soften, may be better. Initial penetration may be difficult or painful and may require lubricant, toys, or dilators. Move gently. If sex causes pain or difficulty, stop.

Clitoral stimulation with fingers, friction, or toys can also produce pleasure and orgasm. Keep body parts and devices that touch the clitoris clean, and clean the perineum after sex to reduce infection risk.

Anal sex remains possible, but carries higher HIV exposure risk. A postoperative vagina may have less elasticity and lubrication than a cisgender vagina, making bleeding or tearing more likely. Use plenty of lubricant and reduce risk with PrEP, condoms, post-exposure prophylaxis when needed, and regular testing.

Postoperative vaginal depth and width vary by body size, technique, available tissue, surgeon skill, and personal goals.

It is not recommended to pursue excessive depth or width. Depending on body size, a very deep vagina may contact other organs during sex or dilation and cause harm. Vaginal size also does not necessarily determine sexual quality.

In every technique, vaginal depth changes slightly from day to day. Do not panic immediately when noticing small changes.

Dilation methods vary substantially between institutions. Some use methods similar to those for congenital vaginal agenesis, with prolonged wearing to reduce infection risk. Others require several short daily sessions reaching full depth to prevent loss of depth.

Dilator schedule and method depend on the surgeon. [Chettasak#通模具]({{< ref "chettawut#通模具" >}}) may be a useful reference.

Some doctors prefer slightly flexible silicone dilators, while others provide glass or rigid plastic dilators. Follow the doctor's recommendation.

If the provided dilator is unsuitable, or if the institution does not provide one, ask the doctor about smooth, seamless, appropriately sized alternatives suitable for medical use.

After one or two months, dilation may become harder because scar tissue begins forming, hardening, and contracting, nerves begin reconnecting, or the initial excitement fades. Continuing dilation while staying relaxed is important. If pain is intolerable, ask the doctor whether something is wrong.

### Preparing for Dilation

External environment:

- Make sure you have enough time
- Make the bed comfortable and slightly elevate the upper body, but not so high that full-depth insertion becomes impossible
- Keep supplies within reach and create a calm environment
- Do not make the preparation so elaborate that dilation feels psychologically exhausting
- Close social media or other distractions when possible
- Disinfect the dilator before use and clean it after use according to medical instructions. Use a condom if appropriate
- Lubricate the dilator thoroughly

Body preparation:

- Wash hands and use medical gloves if needed
- Empty the bowel and bladder
- Lie down and relax
- Apply plenty of lubricant to the vaginal entrance and gently massage. If a finger can be inserted comfortably, gentle massage of the vaginal wall may help relaxation
- If lubricant is thin and hard to push deeper, ask the doctor whether using a vaginal applicator is appropriate

## Prevention and Management of Complications

If discomfort or complications occur outside the hospital, contact the surgeon who performed SRS or the nearest surgeon experienced in SRS postoperative repair whenever possible, unless the problem clearly needs emergency care such as massive bleeding. Doctors without gender-affirming care experience may cause psychological or physical harm. After referral from an SRS surgeon, general specialists such as gynecologists may help with specific problems.

Severe complications are usually managed during hospitalization, but suspected hand or foot numbness, abdominal incision infection, bowel anastomosis infection, adhesions, or bowel obstruction require immediate contact with a doctor.

Urethrovaginal fistula can appear as urine leaking from the vagina. Many cases do not require immediate intervention and may heal, but medical evaluation is needed.

Vaginal granulation tissue is delayed healing and is relatively common. Frequent early dilation can worsen it by repeated trauma. It may improve as dilation frequency decreases. If persistent, follow medical advice regarding cauterization and topical treatment.

Vaginal bleeding is common after surgery, and bleeding around the urethra is also common. It can occur even after the healing period. Avoid constipation and straining. If bleeding occurs, stay calm and still, identify the source and amount, and clean the perineum with water or saline. Determine whether the source is the clitoris, urethra, inside or outside the vagina, or anus. Contact the surgeon for persistent, large-volume, frequent, or bright-red bleeding.

Telogen effluvium is temporary hair loss after stress, shock, or trauma. SRS is invasive and surgical stress may cause shedding. It is usually temporary and hair often grows back.

VTE (deep vein thrombosis): Many clinics require trans women to stop estrogen around surgery, especially if VTE risk is high. HRT type or route, surgical measures, and prolonged bed rest can affect risk. If you object or have high VTE risk, discuss informed consent with the surgeon and ask a clinician about hormone routes with lower thrombosis risk.

After orchiectomy or SRS, testosterone is usually less of a concern, but rarely T (testosterone) or DHT (dihydrotestosterone) can rise through adrenal pathways. If symptoms occur, check free testosterone and DHT and consult a clinician qualified to manage hormone therapy.

After orchiectomy or SRS, estrogen supplementation is still needed to avoid menopausal symptoms such as hot flashes, osteoporosis, and cardiovascular or cerebrovascular risks. Consult a qualified clinician for medication adjustments.

Elevated LH/FSH is common after gonadectomy. Postoperative gonadotropins may sometimes help interpret estrogen status; discuss with a clinician.

In flap-based techniques, the vagina is skin-lined, so there is a risk of skin cancers similar to those of penile or scrotal skin, such as squamous cell carcinoma, basal cell carcinoma, and melanoma. HPV infection may increase risk. Seek evaluation for abnormalities. Other skin diseases can also affect a flap-lined vagina and need similar treatment.

## Q-A

### Q: Do I need to dilate if I do not have penetrative sex?

Doctors give different answers.

1. Some believe dilation is still needed to maintain enough depth for vaginal washing and hygiene
1. Some believe vaginal shrinkage may cause psychological discomfort or risks such as granulation or tissue overgrowth, so they recommend dilation
1. Some suggest considering zero-depth vulvoplasty or other non-depth surgery if depth is not needed
1. Some argue that avoiding vaginal penetration because it is seen as heterosexual sex may reflect heteronormative and cisnormative assumptions, and encourage dilation
1. Some recommend creating a flap-lined vagina even if depth is not needed, but allow stopping dilation if postoperative pain is severe

Some people reject provided dilators because their shape worsens bottom dysphoria or feels unhygienic. Ask the doctor whether you can buy another suitable dilator.

### Q: Can I have a transvaginal examination?

A neovagina in trans women differs from a natal vagina: it has a blind end, lacks a cervix and fornices, and may point more posteriorly.

An anoscope may therefore be more anatomically appropriate for visual examination. It can be inserted, the obturator removed, and the vaginal wall observed as the anoscope is withdrawn.

If a doctor who does not know your history orders a transvaginal examination, they may be trying to examine a uterus or cervix. If cost or examination risk is a concern, explain your anatomy beforehand.
