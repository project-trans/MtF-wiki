## Why SRS? What Can SRS Do? Should I Consider It?

SRS is not required for every transgender person. Its direct effect is to reshape the genitals into a feminine form, provide some functions including penetrative sex, and, in some countries or regions, enable legal gender marker changes.

Its impact on social identity is relatively indirect. When someone is already on effective HRT, SRS or orchiectomy alone may have only limited additional feminizing effects on the endocrine system. Long-term HRT risks are also not necessarily unmanageable under medical supervision.

[WPATH Standards of Care](https://project-trans.org/SOC-8) discuss preoperative HRT and RLE (Real-Life Experience, living in one's affirmed gender), but individual circumstances vary.

Gender dysphoria can point in different directions. Some people care most about social recognition as women; some care most about clothing and external physical feminization; others focus strongly on primary sex characteristics. A person may have dysphoria in all of these areas to different degrees, or may have little or none in some areas.

The need for SRS also varies. Some people cannot accept living in a body without SRS. Some strongly seek it but can live normally for a while. For others, SRS may be an option that makes life more comfortable.

Some people have unrealistic expectations and become disappointed or regretful when SRS does not bring the social transformation they expected. Everyone should understand their own psychological needs and weigh benefits and risks rationally. SRS can directly address primary sex characteristics, but it does not directly resolve every kind of gender dysphoria. Like HRT, the decision is irreversible.

## When SRS?

The only correct answer is “when you are ready,” but readiness is affected by age, law, school, work, finances, family environment, and mental health.

Mental health is important. People with psychotic disorders, severe bipolar disorder with hallucinations or delusions, or severe depression may be advised to wait until symptoms are controlled. SRS requires significant time and effort before and after surgery, including postoperative care such as dilation. If depression causes low motivation that makes postoperative care difficult, surgical outcomes and mental health may worsen. SRS should not be treated as a cure-all for depression related to gender dysphoria; mental health care remains important during preparation.

## Pre- and Postoperative Care Notes

Fasting, hospitalization, bed rest, catheter management, and dilation schedules vary by surgeon and technique. Confirm the details with your surgical team.

Several days of bed rest are often required after surgery, with activity limited to reduce wound separation. After discharge, some people stay nearby for nursing care and dilation training.

Recovery often takes one to three months or longer. Standing, sitting, or walking for too long can increase the risk of wound separation or granulation tissue. Frequent dilation may be required early on; otherwise the risk of stenosis or loss of depth can be high. Some surgeons require daily dilation for one to two years, after which frequency and duration may decrease.

Prolonged bed rest can increase deep vein thrombosis risk. Smoking is an important risk factor, so strict smoking cessation may be required before and after surgery. Estrogen may also increase thrombosis risk, and some doctors ask patients to stop estrogen temporarily. Whether this is necessary for people without other risk factors, and how much psychological harm stopping estrogen may cause, remains debated. Medicines such as aspirin, anticoagulants, and NSAIDs may affect bleeding or inflammation, so discuss them with the doctor.
