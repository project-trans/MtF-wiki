## How to Review Surgical Results

If you want your surgical review to help as many people as possible, follow these suggestions:

1. For any review, whether image or text and whether positive or negative, provide the surgeon's name. If the surgeon is not well known in the community, provide enough identifying information.

1. A helpful postoperative vulva photo should include a clear image and, at minimum, the following context:

   - Surgeon name
   - Year of surgery
   - Surgical technique
   - Time since surgery
   - Anything viewers should pay attention to

   Old photos may not represent a surgeon's current skills or techniques, and early postoperative photos may show excessive swelling.

   Submit to <mtfwiki@project-trans.org>. Members of the {{< mtf-wiki >}} working group will process the material before publication.

1. A helpful written surgical review should include:

   **It is recommended to write the review after enough time has passed for the physical effects to stabilize and for major emotional fluctuation to settle.**

   Surgeon name, year of surgery, time since surgery when writing, and surgical technique.

   In addition, describe the following as objectively as possible:

   - Waiting time and scheduling
   - Perioperative pain level
   - Subjective psychological changes around surgery
   - Labial appearance, vaginal depth, and depth shrinkage
   - Postoperative sexual sensation, recovery time, and complications
   - Surgical cost
   - Hospitalization duration

   Appearance should not be reviewed only as “pretty or not” or by color. Describe concrete details such as:

   - Whether the anatomy is accurate, such as clitoris, clitoral hood, labia minora, and commissures
   - What the scars are like
   - Whether scars are easy to hide
   - Whether there is obvious tension or stretching of flaps

   Submit to <mtfwiki@project-trans.org>. Members of the {{< mtf-wiki >}} working group will process the material before publication.

Note: a surgical review is not merely a personal impression. Different people in the community have different goals for surgery.

In all cases, the prerequisite for a review is that the person who had surgery wants to share it.

## For People Researching Surgery Beforehand

When asking the community about surgery before your own procedure, use the points above to obtain information that is more useful to you.

Do not make hurtful comments about another person's surgical outcome, either to their face or behind their back. Consider whether your questions may hurt them. A casual “that looks bad” can cause long-lasting distress, especially when postoperative mental state may be unstable.
