## SRS Techniques

SRS is often classified by the method used to create the vagina:

1. Flap-based SRS\
   Existing genital skin grafts or flaps are used to form the vagina.\
   Classic flap-based SRS uses penile inversion (PI), where pedicled penile skin is inverted to line the vagina and scrotal skin is used to form external structures. It is relatively straightforward and has fewer severe complications, but anatomical limits may make highly refined external appearance harder to achieve.\
   Other so-called non-PI techniques use free scrotal grafts for the vagina and penile or foreskin flaps for external structures. These can let surgeons create structures closer to those of cisgender women, but recovery is longer, risks such as vaginal stenosis may be higher, and dilation requirements are stricter. Recent PI techniques may also combine scrotal grafts and tissues such as urethral mucosa to improve results.\
   A shared drawback of flap-based SRS is limited vaginal lubrication. Secretions from glands such as the bulbourethral glands and prostate may remain, but often are not enough for use. Flap-based SRS also depends on available tissue. A small original organ, long duration of HRT, early HRT start, circumcision, or prior orchiectomy may cause insufficient material, limited depth, or less ideal appearance.

1. Colon SRS\
   A segment of sigmoid colon is taken through open or laparoscopic surgery and used as the vagina, while external structures are formed from existing genital tissue. Advantages include fewer requirements for original genital tissue, natural secretion, ample depth, and lower risk of stenosis with less dilation. Disadvantages include stronger odor from intestinal tissue, higher cost, more complex surgery, higher complication risk, increased risk of later digestive problems such as bowel obstruction, and abdominal scarring. Colon SRS may also be used for revision when other methods result in stenosis or insufficient depth.

1. SRS using other materials\
   These include peritoneal and biologic graft methods. They may provide more natural secretion and require less original genital tissue, but durability remains uncertain. Peritoneal methods may have significant postoperative shrinkage in some reports.

1. Zero-depth SRS\
   This creates only the vulva without a deep vagina. It is suitable for people who do not need vaginal depth. Compared with full-depth SRS, it is simpler, lower risk, has a much shorter recovery, and does not require dilation.

Regardless of the method used for the vaginal canal, the vulva is usually formed from existing genital tissue and uses existing nerves for sexual sensation. There should not be a fundamental difference in sensation in principle. However, surgeons using the same named technique can still have very different results.

### An Ideal SRS Result Would Achieve

(The following describes an idealized perfect outcome; different surgeons have different strengths.)

- Appearance difficult to distinguish from that of typical women, with complete, clear, and natural labia majora and labia minora
- Labia minora fully surrounding the vaginal opening and vestibule containing the urethral opening, with labia majora outside them
- A natural clitoris connected to the frenulum and covered by a clitoral hood
- Natural posterior fourchette and commissure below the vaginal opening
- Natural tissue color, appropriate distance from the anus, and natural horizontal and vertical positioning
- Good sensation in the clitoris and labia minora, normal clitoral engorgement, and no excess erectile tissue around the vulva or urethral opening
- Ability to orgasm from clitoral stimulation and potentially from internal prostate stimulation
- Vaginal depth and opening structure that allow penetrative sex naturally, without excess tissue
- No severe complications or other problems, and no pain or friction discomfort in daily life or sex
- No hair growth inside the vagina, on the inner labia, or in the vestibule

Even highly experienced surgeons cannot guarantee perfect results. SRS is complex and can have serious complications, including vaginal stenosis, shrinkage or prolapse, vaginal or vulvar tears, bowel-vaginal or urethral-vaginal fistula, urethral stenosis, labial or clitoral necrosis, nerve damage with loss of sensitivity, deep vein thrombosis, persistent bleeding, granulation tissue, and inflammation. When researching surgeons, consider both positive and negative reports.
