Cyproterone acetate (CPA) is a commonly used antiandrogen. It is used in treatment of prostate cancer and androgen-related symptoms such as acne and hair loss, and is one of the most widely used antiandrogens in feminizing hormone therapy[^5] [^6] [^8]. Common brand names include Androcur<sup>&reg;</sup> and Siterone<sup>&reg;</sup>.

CPA has very strong progestogenic activity and suppresses ovarian function[^15]. It is also used in combined contraceptives such as Diane-35.

## Route and Dosage

{{< notice warning >}}
This section applies only to transfeminine people who are also using estrogen preparations.
{{< /notice >}}

Data on long-term CPA monotherapy in transgender and gender-diverse populations are limited and need further study. People considering this should seek professional medical guidance.

With CPA monotherapy, even the maximum recommended dose (10-12.5 mg/day) cannot fully suppress testosterone[^1]; this is achieved only when combined with estrogen[^2].

- Route: oral, with no obvious taste.
- Recommended dose: **10-12.5 mg/day**[^8]
  - Multiple studies have found that, when combined with estradiol, around 10 mg/day maximally suppresses testosterone, with effects similar to higher doses[^2]. WPATH SOC-8 also recommends 10 mg/day[^8].
  - Higher doses increase adverse effects and health risks; lower doses may not suppress testosterone adequately.
- Further research found that an ultra-low dose of 12.5 mg twice weekly (equivalent to about 3.5 mg/day) can also sufficiently suppress testosterone[^10]. **After the recommended dose is effective, reduce as much as possible to 5-6.25 mg/day.** Because evidence is limited, reduction below 5 mg/day is not currently recommended.
- If serum testosterone remains high at the recommended dose (10 mg/day), first check whether estradiol concentration is high enough, and consult a relevant clinician when needed.
- Appropriate vitamin B<sub>12</sub> supplementation is recommended.
- Taper gradually when stopping.

## Side Effects

- **Meningioma risk**: CPA may induce meningioma, with risk positively related to dose and duration. Use the lowest practical dose. If cumulative dose is high (over 10 g), MRI screening is recommended[^7].
- **Psychiatric effects**: CPA may increase depression risk. Possible mechanisms include antiandrogenic and potential glucocorticoid effects in people with male physiology, and vitamin B<sub>12</sub> deficiency[^4]. Vitamin B<sub>12</sub> deficiency is associated with multiple psychiatric and mood changes, including depression[^3]. Appropriate vitamin B<sub>12</sub> supplementation is needed.
- **Hypertension and hyperglycemia**: CPA may increase insulin resistance and raise blood glucose[^9]. People with diabetes should use it cautiously; antidiabetic or insulin requirements may change. It is contraindicated when vascular complications are present[^6].
- **Thrombosis and breast cancer risk**: risk may increase to some extent when combined with estrogen[^12] [^13]. Higher thrombosis risk with CPA monotherapy has also been reported[^14].
- **High prolactin**: CPA can markedly increase prolactin in a dose-dependent manner and may cause **prolactinoma** in severe cases. Monitor prolactin regularly[^2].
- **Hepatotoxicity**: the most serious adverse effect of CPA, dose-related, presenting as elevated liver enzymes. Severe cases can cause **liver failure** or **death**[^2] [^5] [^6]. Check **liver function** before and during use[^5].
- {{< tag/neg "Unclear" >}} Early introduction of CPA, especially at high doses, may impair breast development[^11].
