## Introduction

GnRH analogues are used for puberty suppression. They include agonists and antagonists that act on pituitary GnRH receptors. Although agonists initially increase hormone levels, with continued treatment both types have comparable efficacy and keep hormones in the prepubertal range[^1] [^2] [^3].

GnRH analogues are routinely used for central precocious puberty and have good reversibility and safety. In transgender children, this treatment is sometimes described as a "pause button" for puberty. It pauses development of pubertal secondary sex characteristics, such as breast development, pubic hair growth, menstruation, penile and scrotal growth, erections, and skeletal feminization or masculinization. This gives transgender minors more time to explore themselves and think more maturely about gender identity. If they still want transition as adults, gender-affirming hormone therapy can follow; if they decide not to transition, the original puberty resumes after stopping. International studies suggest this treatment can improve mental health related to gender dysphoria in transgender adolescents[^1] [^2].

However, it does not directly change gender identity, does not directly relieve or treat other primary psychological conditions such as gender dysphoria, and does not reverse already-developed secondary sex characteristics such as Adam's apple, voice, or skeleton.

Over the past decade, clinicians have increasingly recognized the distress of adolescents with gender dysphoria or gender incongruence. In some forms of gender dysphoria or incongruence, psychological intervention may be useful and sufficient. For many adolescents, however, pubertal physical changes are intolerable.

Compared with starting gender-affirming treatment long after early puberty, puberty suppression in early puberty may produce better psychological and physical outcomes[^1].

1. Adolescents who meet diagnostic criteria for gender dysphoria or gender incongruence, meet treatment criteria, and request treatment should first receive treatment to suppress pubertal development.
1. Puberty suppression is recommended after the first physical signs of puberty appear.

For transgender minors, GnRH analogues may be used for puberty suppression after evaluation by a mental-health clinician and guardian consent, giving them time to think and explore themselves.

## Side Effects

1. During use, bone-mass accrual is affected, bone density may be lower than peers of the same sex assigned at birth, and height growth may slow. Literature reports that this effect can gradually disappear after stopping, although bone density may remain low if later hormone therapy is inadequate[^1] [^2]. Calcium and vitamin D supplementation are therefore appropriate[^1].
1. Continued penile and scrotal growth is suppressed, which may potentially affect future surgery because available local flap area may be smaller than usual.
1. Unknown effects on brain development, such as intelligence and cognitive development[^1]. Recent research suggests cognitive ability is not obviously affected[^4].
1. Other possible side effects include sensitivity, irritability, agitation, injection-site pain, acne, rash, headache, and weight gain[^5].

It is important to note that children and adolescents are still growing and developing. While monitoring pubertal changes such as sex characteristics and sex-hormone levels, growth should also be regularly assessed, including height, bone mass, and nutritional status. Monitoring and guidance by an experienced multidisciplinary team, including endocrinology, pediatrics, and mental-health specialists, is recommended.
