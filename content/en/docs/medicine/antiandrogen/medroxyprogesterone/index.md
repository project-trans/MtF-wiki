Medroxyprogesterone acetate (MPA) is a steroidal progestin with antigonadotropic activity that can suppress androgen levels[^1]. It can be used as an alternative when [cyproterone acetate]({{< ref cyproterone >}}) is unavailable, but is somewhat less effective[^2] [^3] [^11]. For testosterone suppression by MPA, see [below]({{< ref "#testosterone-suppression" >}}).

## Route and Dosage {#usage}

- Route: oral.
- Recommended dose: **not yet validated**, including:
  - As a progesterone substitute: 5-10 mg[^9] {{< tag/neg "Antiandrogenic effect may be weak" >}}
  - As an antiandrogen: estimated 30-100 mg[^12]; see below.
- Use the lowest dose that maintains the desired effect.
- Taper gradually when stopping.

### MPA Dose and Testosterone Levels {#testosterone-suppression}

- **Adding MPA (5-10 mg/day, sublingual or intramuscular) to estradiol plus spironolactone treatment in MtF people reduced mean testosterone to 0.79 &plusmn; 0.18 ng/mL**[^3], slightly above the premenopausal female reference range (0.1-0.55 ng/mL)[^5]. However, among this site's readers, inadequate testosterone suppression (1.68 ng/mL) has been reported with MPA 5-10 mg plus estradiol valerate 4 mg.
- In men treated long-term with MPA for sexual drive (intramuscular 300-400 mg/week, equivalent to 43-57 mg/day), testosterone suppression was maximal when blood concentration exceeded 25 ng/mL, with suppression of about 87-91%; suppression persisted after stopping while concentrations remained above 20 ng/mL[^12]. Based on those data, injection doses above 10 mg/day could maintain this effect. Oral absorption is low, so oral doses would need to be several times higher.
- Intramuscular MPA 5-10 mg/day for male sexual drive reduced testosterone by an average of 31% (range 6-63%), to about 0.26-4.35 ng/mL[^6].
- Oral MPA 60 mg/day for paraphilias reduced testosterone by 50-75% within three months, to 1-4 ng/mL[^7].
- Oral MPA 30 mg/day for androgen excess with hirsutism in women reduced mean testosterone from 1.33 ng/mL to 0.57 ng/mL, about a 57% decrease[^8]. Note that the baseline was still far below the adult male reference range[^5].

## Side Effects {#adverse-reactions}

- **Thromboembolic disease**: may cause thromboembolism and thrombotic disease, such as thrombophlebitis, pulmonary embolism, cerebrovascular insufficiency, coronary occlusion, retinal thrombosis, and mesenteric thrombosis[^1] [^4].
- **Depression**: MPA may increase depression risk[^4] [^13].
- **Liver effects**: may cause liver dysfunction, such as jaundice. Check **liver function** before use and regularly during use; if such effects occur or are suspected, stop and reassess risk[^1] [^13].
- **Breast cancer risk**: risk may increase to some extent when combined with estradiol[^4] [^13].
- **Vision effects**: unexplained sudden or gradual partial or complete vision loss, proptosis, diplopia, or migraine may occur. If papilledema or retinal vascular lesions are diagnosed, stop MPA[^4] [^10] [^13].
- Adrenal suppression: MPA may cause adrenal suppression and may affect carbohydrate metabolism, but does not cause diabetes[^1].
- Skin and androgenic symptoms: MPA still has weak androgenic and glucocorticoid potency and may cause acne, body-hair growth, hair loss, and other androgen-sensitive symptoms[^1] [^11]. Urticaria, itching, edema, rash, and other allergic reactions are also occasionally reported[^10] [^13].
- Weight changes[^4] [^13]
- Fluid retention[^4] [^10] [^13]

## Composition {#descriptions}

- Active ingredient: Medroxyprogesterone Acetate
- Chemical name: 6α-methyl-17α-hydroxypregn-4-ene-3,20-dione acetate
- Molecular formula: C<sub>24</sub>H<sub>34</sub>O<sub>4</sub>
- Molecular weight: 386.53
- CAS RN: [71-58-9](https://webbook.nist.gov/cgi/cbook.cgi?ID=71-58-9)
- Bioavailability: 0.6-10%[^10]
  Mean steady-state blood concentration during continuous oral 10 mg use: 0.71 ng/mL[^13]
