## Basics

### Introduction

This page explains how to choose between intramuscular and subcutaneous injection, how to choose syringes, how to self-inject, and what reactions may occur afterward.

Self-injection is a common medication route, and most people can learn to do it safely and effectively.

### Should I choose intramuscular or subcutaneous injection?

Long-acting medicines usually use intramuscular injection. Sometimes, however, subcutaneous injection is easier.

- Intramuscular injection
  - Most long-acting medicines, including hormones and antipsychotics, commonly use intramuscular injection.
  - Muscle has much better blood supply than fat. This helps the carrier oil and dissolved medicine absorb faster and more steadily.
  - Larger muscles, such as the gluteus maximus and vastus lateralis, can hold much more fluid than fat and are less likely to hurt after injection.
  - Try not to exceed 3 mL at one site.
  - Because the site is deeper, use a safe, reachable area with fewer nerves.
  - More severe allergic reactions are less likely, and reactions tend to fade faster.
- Subcutaneous injection
  - Simple and easy.
  - Some studies of testosterone and long-acting antipsychotics suggest absorption may not differ substantially.
  - It comfortably holds less fluid. Larger injection volumes may cause pain.
  - Try not to exceed 1.5 mL at one site. If the volume is larger, split it into two injections.
  - Drug release is smoother and level fluctuation is slightly smaller.
  - Absorption is slower. If you are allergic to the carrier oil, the body may take longer to clear the allergen, causing longer itching and swelling. {{< tag/pos "Allergy is uncommon." >}}

If this is your first injection, subcutaneous injection may be easier. If you trust your technique or have an allergic constitution, intramuscular injection may be preferable.

### Basic hygiene principles

References: [WHO multilingual toolkit](https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9789241599252), [CDC infection control](https://restoredcdc.org/www.cdc.gov/infection-control/hcp/core-practices/index.html), [CDC injection safety](https://restoredcdc.org/www.cdc.gov/injection-safety/hcp/clinical-safety/index.html)

- Do not touch disinfected surfaces or skin with your hands. If you accidentally touch them, disinfect again and wait until fully dry.
- After disinfecting skin or a rubber stopper with alcohol, let it air-dry for about 30 seconds. Do not blow, wipe, or fan it dry.
- During use, needles and syringes must not touch any non-sterile surface. If contamination is **suspected**, discard the whole syringe set and the medicine in it. {{< tag/pos "Discard the whole set if contamination is suspected" >}}
- Equipment used in a body, or contaminated in any other way, must not touch medicine or pierce a rubber stopper again, even if the needle is changed.
- Syringes and needles are disposable. If contamination is suspected or after use in a body, discard the whole set immediately and do not reuse it.
- Regardless of gloves or alcohol, strictly wash hands with soap and running water. **Disinfection does not replace handwashing.**

## Choosing syringes

### Syringe choice

Common needle-and-syringe sets include:

- 1 mL, 0.8-1.2 cm / 0.35-0.5 mm (non-removable insulin needle, low dead space)
- 1 mL, 1.6 cm / 0.45 mm (low dead space)
- 2 mL, 2.0 cm / 0.5 mm
- 2 mL, 2.5 cm / 0.6 mm
- 5 mL, 3.2 cm / 0.7 mm
- 10 mL, 3.2 cm / 0.8 mm

Dead space is the space trapped at the syringe-needle connection. Low-dead-space syringes are designed to reduce medicine wasted there.

Rules:\
If your medicine is in a multi-use rubber-stopper vial, use a 1 mL syringe for accurate dosing.\
For intramuscular injection, use a needle with adequate length and gauge.\
If your BMI is high, you may need a longer needle around 3.2 cm.

``` csv
,Intramuscular injection,Subcutaneous injection
Single-use ampoule,longer needle,appropriate short needle
Multi-use vial,1 mL syringe plus longer needle,1 mL low-dead-space needle or insulin needle
```

### Getting safe, hygienic syringes

Use sterile, reliable syringes and needles from local pharmacies, medical supply stores, or healthcare services. Avoid suspiciously cheap or unknown products.

## Injection tutorial

Recommended videos: [VA intramuscular injection, English](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fMEZdNodGM0), [Hong Kong Hemophilia Society subcutaneous injection, Cantonese](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=s_20-g15v54)\
References: [Fenway Health intramuscular and subcutaneous injection guide](https://fenwayhealth.org/wp-content/uploads/MG-6_TransHealth_InjectionGuide.pdf), [opening ampoules](https://www.tcichemicals.com/US/en/product/container/ampule), [opening ampoules](https://www.bristol.ac.uk/media-library/sites/vetscience/documents/clinical-skills/How%20to%20Open%20a%20Glass%20Vial.pdf)

### 1. Prepare the workspace

You will need alcohol swabs, sterile cotton balls, a bandage, one or two syringes, and the medicine. Put them on a relatively clean surface.

### 2. Hygiene: wash hands

Wash your hands with soap and running water. This is important for infection prevention.

### 3. Open the medicine

#### If it is a single-use ampoule

Flick the ampoule so the liquid above the neck moves downward. Check whether the neck has a score mark; if it does, the dot on the cap usually marks the scored side. Hold the ampoule through tissue, keep both hands in line with the ampoule, face the score outward, and snap it away from you. {{< tag/pos "Most ampoules are pre-scored" >}}

Ampoule medicines must not be reused. If your dose is less than one ampoule, discard the remainder.

#### If it is a rubber-stopper vial

For first use, remove the cap. Check whether the vial is multi-use or single-use. Before use, wipe the stopper with an alcohol swab and wait until it dries before drawing medicine. {{< tag/neg "Do not wipe or blow it dry" >}}

### 4. Intramuscular injection: switch to a long needle

Open the syringe package. Do not remove the needle cover. Remove the needle from the syringe, attach the long needle firmly, and avoid touching the syringe connector. If you touch it, discard the whole set. For subcutaneous injection, a short needle can be used directly.

### 5. Draw the medicine

#### Ampoule

Open the syringe, remove the needle cover, and avoid touching the ampoule mouth with the needle. Draw the medicine. If there are large bubbles, turn the needle upward, flick the syringe gently, and push the bubbles out.

#### Rubber-stopper vial

Draw the dose you need. For example, if the vial is 40 mg/mL and you need 8 mg, draw `8 / 40 = 0.2 mL`. Draw an equal volume of air into the syringe, insert the needle vertically, inject the air above the liquid surface, invert the vial, and draw the medicine. Remove bubbles and adjust to the needed volume before removing the needle.

#### Draw a tiny amount of air

If the syringe is not low-dead-space, you may draw a tiny amount of air to compensate for dead space and keep too much medicine from remaining at the needle-syringe connection. {{< tag/pos "A tiny amount of air injected intramuscularly or subcutaneously is usually harmless" >}}

Dangerous air embolism generally requires a large amount of air entering a vein.

### 6. Find the injection site

#### Intramuscular injection

Use your thigh. Looking straight at the thigh, imagine dividing it into thirds horizontally and vertically. Choose the outer column of the middle third. This is the vastus lateralis, which is easy to reach, large enough for more fluid, and less likely to hit major nerves or vessels.

#### Subcutaneous injection

Use the front of the thigh or the abdomen at least 5 cm away from the navel. Avoid inflamed skin, wounds, scars, warts, and moles. Rotate injection sites.

### 7. Disinfect the injection site

Use an alcohol swab in a spiral from the center outward, then let it air-dry. Do not wipe or blow it dry.

### 8. Inject

Hold the syringe like a pen. Insert the needle quickly and decisively. {{< tag/neg "Slower insertion hurts more" >}}

#### Intramuscular injection

Relax the muscle. Hold the syringe with one hand and gently stretch the skin and fat layer with the other. Insert perpendicular to the site. If the needle is 2.5 cm or shorter, insert it fully; if longer, insert about 2.5 cm. If BMI is high, a 3.2 cm needle may need to be inserted fully.

#### Subcutaneous injection

Pinch about 3 cm of skin without pinching too tightly. With a longer needle, a 45° angle may be easier; otherwise inject vertically. Insert about 1 cm vertically or 1.5 cm at 45°.

#### Intramuscular injection: check for no blood return

Pull back *gently* for 5-10 seconds. You should not see blood. If blood returns, you may be in a vein; remove the needle and try again. {{< tag/neg "Thick, water-insoluble oily medicines may embolize vessels" >}}

Aspiration guidance is debated, but this document recommends checking as a precaution. If you forget after starting, do not panic; a true fully intravenous injection in the middle vastus lateralis is unlikely.

#### Push the medicine

Push the plunger until the medicine is delivered. If the medicine has just come from the refrigerator, it may hurt slightly; pushing more slowly can help. Wait 10-15 seconds, remove the needle, and press with sterile cotton for several seconds. A small leak of medicine or blood is usually fine. Do not massage the injection site.

### 9. Dispose of the needle

Use a sharps container if available, and follow local disposal rules or healthcare guidance. If you must recap, the one-hand scoop method can reduce needle-stick risk. Reference: [one-hand scoop video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bEeAo2jCJjw)

Do not try to break, bend, or disassemble the needle before disposal.

## Reactions after injection

### What is normal?

- Pain when pressing or rubbing a subcutaneous site for one or two days
- Muscle soreness for one or two days after intramuscular injection
- A small, mild lump at the injection site, usually softening by itself within weeks

### What may suggest allergy?

- Mild redness, itching, or warmth around a subcutaneous injection site
- A larger lump gradually appearing one or two weeks after subcutaneous injection
- Clear muscle soreness lasting one or two weeks after intramuscular injection
- A previous site suddenly reacting when you take the next injection, suggesting delayed allergy

Allergy after intramuscular injection may be much less visible than after subcutaneous injection.

### Allergy: should I see a doctor?

- If a lump keeps expanding
- If a lump is attached to the skin and hard to move
- If skin over a lump turns bluish-purple
- If the skin itches, is tender, or seems close to breaking down
- If signs of delayed allergy do not improve by themselves
- If you are unsure whether it is allergy or infection {{< tag/pos "Infection is rare" >}}

Try to see a doctor. If you have systemic symptoms such as trouble breathing, fever, widespread rash, throat swelling, or lip swelling, seek medical care immediately. {{< tag/neg "Staying alive comes first" >}}

### What may indicate infection?

Infection is rare, but if it occurs it needs prompt treatment.

- Redness, swelling, and heat at the injection site
- Redness around the site that expands rapidly
- Spontaneous pain, worsening pain, or severe tenderness
- Linear red streaking
- Fever {{< tag/neg "Go to the hospital immediately" >}}
- Swelling that feels fluid-filled, or pus discharge
- Recurrent or persistent low fever, unexplained low fever, muscle swelling, muscle pain, or stiffness

If any of these occur, seek emergency care. {{< tag/neg "Do not self-medicate blindly with antibiotics" >}}

In general, allergy is mainly itchy, while infection usually hurts more and progresses quickly.

### What to say at the hospital

As much as safely possible, tell the doctor medically relevant information: actual injection site, timing, symptom progression, hygiene conditions, and the medicine used. Deep infection is very rare, but dangerous if it happens.
