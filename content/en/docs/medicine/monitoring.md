Regular monitoring during GAHT helps confirm treatment effect and improve safety. This page does not apply to people using puberty blockers; follow the specialist team's protocol in that situation.

{{< notice info "Tip" >}}
Laboratories may use different units. Use this site's hormone unit converter when needed.
{{< /notice >}}

## What to Check and When {#when-and-how}

Usually monitored:

- **Sex hormones**, especially estradiol, testosterone, and prolactin. Blood should usually be drawn near the next scheduled dose rather than immediately after taking medication. After a complete hormone panel has been checked, follow-up may focus on the key items. Ask the clinician whether fasting is needed.
- **Liver function.** Check once before treatment and about every three months during treatment. Monitor more closely during the first six months of cyproterone acetate or bicalutamide use.

Consider monitoring when clinically relevant:

- **Fasting glucose and insulin**, and sometimes HbA1c, because estrogens and some antiandrogens may affect glucose tolerance.
- **Fasting lipids**, especially LDL cholesterol and triglycerides. High triglycerides are associated with rare pancreatitis risk and may relate to thyroid function.
- **Bone mineral density**, especially when estradiol and testosterone are both low for a long time.
- **Kidney function**, especially when using spironolactone or when kidney disease is present.
- **Electrolytes**, especially potassium, sodium, calcium, and magnesium when using spironolactone or interacting medications.
- **Coagulation status**, especially before surgery, during prolonged immobilization, or with high-dose estrogen/progestogen use.
- **Complete blood count**, especially red blood cells and platelets.
- **Thyroid function**, especially when using thyroid hormone replacement.
- **Breast screening**, following the same general screening guidance used for comparable cisgender populations and individualized risk.
- **Brain MRI** when long-term or high-dose cyproterone acetate raises concern for meningioma.
- **Body weight and body fat**, because GAHT commonly changes fat distribution and total body weight.

## How to Read Key Values {#what-to-check}

For people who have not had orchiectomy or genital surgery that removes the testes:

- **Estradiol**: maintain within the adult female range used by the lab or guideline.
- **Testosterone**: when using cyproterone acetate or similar gonadal-suppressive drugs, aim near the adult female range.
- **FSH and LH**: may be low when gonadal function is suppressed.
- **Prolactin**: monitor especially when using cyproterone acetate; investigate marked elevation.

After orchiectomy or genital surgery that removes the testes:

- **Estradiol**: maintain within the adult female range.
- **FSH and LH**: may rise after gonad removal; very high values can suggest insufficient estradiol.

Other indicators should generally remain within the lab's normal reference range.

{{< notice warning "Caution" >}}
Reference ranges differ by laboratory, assay method, and unit. The values below are for education only. Ask a qualified professional to interpret your results.
{{< /notice >}}

## Estradiol (E2) {#e2}

Estradiol is the main estrogen used in GAHT. It supports feminizing changes and affects breast tissue, fat tissue, bone, and other organs.

Testing is usually done near the next scheduled dose.

{{< notice warning "Transdermal Products" >}}
If using estradiol gel or another transdermal product, do not apply it to the blood-draw arm before testing. Contamination can produce falsely high, clinically useless estradiol results.
{{< /notice >}}

- Adult female follicular range: about **30-100 pg/mL**.
- Adult female luteal range: about **70-300 pg/mL**.
- GAHT except injections: average **100-200 pg/mL** is often targeted; trough **55-150 pg/mL** may be used, with values above 100 pg/mL often preferred.
- Estradiol injections: trough values above **200 pg/mL** are sometimes used for monotherapy, but require risk-aware monitoring.

## Testosterone (T) {#t}

Testosterone is the main androgen. It acts directly through androgen receptors, locally as dihydrotestosterone (DHT), and indirectly after aromatization to estradiol.

Spironolactone and bicalutamide do not reliably lower testosterone levels, so testosterone labs do not directly show their receptor-blocking effect. Cyproterone acetate and other progestogenic or gonadal-suppressive drugs can lower testosterone; lowering below the adult female range is usually unnecessary. After orchiectomy, remaining testosterone mainly comes from the adrenal cortex.

- Adult female testosterone range: **0.1-0.55 ng/mL**.
- Adult male testosterone range: **2.64-9.16 ng/mL**.

## FSH and LH {#fsh-lh}

FSH and LH are pituitary gonadotropins regulated by GnRH. They participate in gonadal function and are suppressed by cyproterone acetate or sufficiently high estradiol. After orchiectomy they can rise; very high levels may suggest insufficient estradiol replacement.

- Adult premenopausal female FSH: **1.8-11.2 mIU/mL**.
- Postmenopausal FSH: **30-120 mIU/mL**.
- Adult premenopausal female LH: follicular **2.0-9.0 mIU/mL**, luteal **2.0-11.0 mIU/mL**.
- Postmenopausal LH: **20.0-70.0 mIU/mL**.

## Progesterone (P4) {#p4}

Progesterone is the main natural progestogen. Its role in transfeminine GAHT remains unclear, and oral progesterone can cause neurosteroid-like sedating effects.

## Prolactin (PRL) {#prl}

Prolactin is a pituitary hormone involved in breast development and lactation. Estrogens and progestogens can increase prolactin, and cyproterone acetate can have a notable effect. Stress, breast stimulation, and some dopamine D2 receptor antagonist drugs can also increase prolactin.

- Adult female reference range: **4.79-23.3 ng/mL**.
- If prolactin is more than about three times the upper limit, consider close follow-up, dose reduction, medication change, or evaluation for prolactinoma, such as brain MRI, with professional guidance.

## Liver Function (LFT) {#lft}

Many medications are metabolized by the liver, including estrogens and antiandrogens. Bicalutamide and cyproterone acetate have reports of acute liver failure, and severe liver disease is a contraindication for several GAHT medicines.

Check liver function before treatment and about every three months during treatment. During the first six months of cyproterone acetate or bicalutamide, monthly checks may be appropriate. Pay particular attention to transaminases. If abnormalities appear, reassess the regimen and monitor until recovery under medical care.
