## Introduction

Raloxifene is a commonly used SERM. As a partial agonist of ER&alpha;, full antagonist of ER&beta;, and agonist of GPER (G protein-coupled estrogen receptor), it has estrogenic effects on liver metabolism, skin, bone, and fat distribution, and anti-estrogenic effects on breast development, vaginal dryness, and hot flashes. It has no antigonadotropic action. In cisgender women, raloxifene is mainly used for prevention of postmenopausal osteoporosis and breast cancer[^4]. In transgender people, it may provide feminizing effects while inhibiting breast development[^3].

## Adverse Reactions and Contraindications

The clinical trials that produced the following information were mainly conducted in postmenopausal cisgender women, but the information still has some reference value.

According to prescribing information[^9], raloxifene adverse reactions and contraindications include:

1. Thrombosis and stroke: raloxifene increases the risk of thrombosis and stroke. Use cautiously in patients with atrial fibrillation, hypertension, smoking, or a history of stroke.
1. Liver injury: raloxifene should be used cautiously in patients with liver injury. According to LiverTox, raloxifene is a possible rare cause of clinically significant liver injury. In large clinical trials, serum liver enzyme elevations were not more frequent than placebo, but isolated liver injury cases attributed to raloxifene have appeared in large-scale use[^10], possibly related to drug interactions.
1. Hot flashes: raloxifene increases hot-flash incidence. In clinical trials for osteoporosis prevention, about one quarter of volunteers receiving raloxifene experienced hot flashes, compared with one sixth in controls.
1. Leg cramps: raloxifene increases leg-cramp incidence. In osteoporosis treatment trials, incidence was 7.0% vs 3.7% (raloxifene vs placebo); in osteoporosis prevention trials, incidence was 5.9% vs 1.9%.
1. Drug interactions: review any concurrent medication with a clinician or pharmacist, especially drugs that affect coagulation, liver metabolism, or sex-hormone-related risks.

Raloxifene also reduces hepatic IGF-1 synthesis, causing GH to increase by feedback, and antagonizes estrogen's antigonadotropic action. This ultimately increases testosterone levels in people with testes[^1] [^2].

## Raloxifene HRT Regimen

Raloxifene dosage should be 60 mg daily.

Unlike estradiol, raloxifene has no antigonadotropic effect[^4], so it cannot be combined with progestogens to fully lower androgen levels. Progestogens alone reduce testosterone by only about 50-70%, and testosterone may partially rebound during long-term use[^6]. Spironolactone cannot effectively antagonize high androgen levels[^7]. Therefore, ordinary antiandrogen strategies such as CPA or spironolactone, which both need estrogen coadministration, do not apply in this regimen.

Effective antiandrogen strategies include:

* One option is low-dose cyproterone acetate (6.25-12.5 mg) plus bicalutamide (50-100 mg). Although pharmacologically plausible, this regimen lacks large studies and trials, so it should be used cautiously.
* Another option is high-dose bicalutamide (150-200 mg) to antagonize androgen receptors. Because this can raise testosterone by feedback and increase estradiol produced by aromatization, it is unclear whether 60 mg raloxifene is sufficient. Splitting 60 mg into morning and evening doses may be tried. This regimen should also be used cautiously.<details>A study in older men with prostate cancer showed that 20 mg tamoxifen only prevented most breast development caused by high-dose bicalutamide[^5]. However, the volunteers were mainly older men, meaning they had lower sex-hormone levels. Research also suggests that, for breast-cancer prevention, 60 mg raloxifene is less effective than 20 mg tamoxifen[^8].</details>
* GnRH analogues can also be used to suppress androgens, though this is relatively costly. Orchiectomy is another option.

Estrogen and raloxifene should not be used together. Raloxifene already has estrogenic effects on bone, skin, and fat distribution. Combining estrogen with raloxifene would require extremely high raloxifene doses to suppress breast development, substantially increasing liver-related effects on coagulation and lipid metabolism without providing additional feminization in other respects.
