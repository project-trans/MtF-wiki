This section mainly describes a class of HRT medications that may be relevant for non-binary people and people who temporarily do not want breast development. People using standard HRT generally do not need these medications.

## Breast Development

Research suggests that breast-development extent is largely unrelated to external factors such as age. Different HRT regimens mainly affect the speed of development, not the final result (from 4.1±2.9 cm to 7.9±3.1 cm after one year)[^1].

## Selective Estrogen Receptor Modulators (SERMs)

Selective estrogen receptor modulators (SERMs) are medications that act on estrogen receptors (ERs). Because each SERM molecule has a different structure and profile, and because ER&alpha;/&beta; mRNA is spliced differently in different tissues, different SERMs can induce distinct receptor conformations after binding to different ERs. This changes affinity for coactivators and corepressors, producing estrogenic effects in some tissues and anti-estrogenic effects in others[^3] [^10]. Clinically, SERMs are used for treatment of ER+ breast cancer, prevention of breast cancer, and prevention of osteoporosis in postmenopausal women[^3].

In short, SERMs can inhibit breast development while acting estrogenically in other respects.

## SERMs for HRT

The only SERM that is generally relevant for HRT regimens is **raloxifene**. Other SERMs are not recommended.
<details>
<summary>Why</summary>
Currently available SERMs include clomifene, tamoxifen, toremifene, raloxifene, lasofoxifene, ospemifene, and bazedoxifene[^3].

Long-term tamoxifen use can cause liver injury and fatty liver, and tamoxifen and many of its active metabolites have very long elimination half-lives, so drug concentrations take a long time to reach steady state. It also does not help improve skin condition[^5] [^4] [^11] [^12].

Clomifene is mainly used clinically for infertility treatment and can greatly increase testosterone levels[^14] [^15], making it unsuitable for HRT. It also has a low probability of causing liver enzyme elevations and a very low probability of severe liver injury[^20].

Long-term toremifene use[^7] [^8] may also cause liver injury or fatty liver.

Lasofoxifene might better feminize skin and fat distribution[^11] [^13], but it is not widely marketed and has no generic product[^21].
</details>

Important points:

1. SERMs have estrogenic effects on fat distribution and bone[^11], so SERMs should not be used together with estrogen. Combining them would only require a larger SERM dose to inhibit breast development[^16], substantially increasing cardiovascular and lipid-metabolic risk.
1. Antiandrogen therapy while using SERMs: because SERMs reduce hepatic IGF-1 synthesis, GH rises by feedback. SERMs also lack antigonadotropic action and block estrogen's antigonadotropic action; in people with testes, both effects can increase testosterone levels[^17] [^18] [^19]. Therefore, effective androgen suppression may require higher-dose bicalutamide, bicalutamide plus low-dose CPA, or GnRHa medications. Evidence is limited, so medical supervision and dose adjustment based on hormone testing are recommended when possible.
1. SERMs affect the liver similarly to synthetic estrogens that resist hepatic metabolism, so attention should be paid to effects on lipid metabolism and coagulation[^5] [^6]. Regular coagulation and lipid testing is recommended.
