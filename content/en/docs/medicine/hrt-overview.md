## Gender-Affirming Hormone Therapy {#intro}

On this site, HRT usually refers to **transgender hormone therapy**. The more current term is **Gender-Affirming Hormone Therapy (GAHT)**. For transfeminine people, GAHT uses estrogens, sometimes progestogens, and antiandrogens to bring hormone levels into a feminizing range, promote feminizing and demasculinizing changes, interrupt further masculinization, and relieve gender dysphoria or gender incongruence. It may also improve quality of life and mental health. Current evidence indicates that hormone therapy is generally safe when guided by qualified clinicians and appropriate risk management.

Hormone therapy is not required for every transfeminine person. For those who need it, however, it can be a major part of self-acceptance and transition.

### Before Starting {#top-notes}

Hormones are not magic. Before starting treatment, it is important to understand both the goals and the limits of GAHT, especially after endogenous puberty has already occurred.

Some mature masculinized traits, such as skeletal structure, vocal pitch, and established body-hair distribution, are difficult to reverse with hormones alone. For some people, the psychological benefit of hormone therapy may be greater than the visible physical change. If needed, non-hormonal interventions such as facial feminization surgery or voice training/surgery may be considered.

Medical transition should be based on personal needs rather than pressure from other people's experiences. Evidence suggests that regret after adult hormone therapy is uncommon, and most people who use puberty blockers proceed to subsequent hormone therapy. Still, a minority of people do not need hormonal transition. Consider GAHT according to your own needs and seek professional medical support when possible.

## Sex Hormones {#sex-hormones}

The main sex-hormone groups are estrogens, progestogens, and androgens. All are present in everyone, while gonadal function determines which group is dominant.

- **Estradiol** (E<sub>2</sub>) is the primary estrogen.
- **Progesterone** (P<sub>4</sub>) is the primary natural progestogen.
- **Testosterone** (T) is the primary androgen.

Estrogens mainly promote feminization, including breast development, softer skin, feminized fat distribution, and support for bone health. Progestogens do not have a clearly established role in feminization or puberty development for transfeminine GAHT. Androgens promote masculinization, including penile growth, broader shoulders and ribs, muscle gain, voice deepening, body-hair growth, and inhibition of estrogen-driven breast development. Androgens also help maintain bone health.

Sex hormones also affect cognition, mood, behavior, and libido. Sufficient estrogen or androgen levels suppress gonadotropins, especially luteinizing hormone (LH) and follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH), which reduces gonadal hormone production. Even after strong gonadal suppression or genital surgery, a low baseline testosterone level may remain because the adrenal cortex also produces small amounts of sex hormones.

## Reference Ranges {#reference-ranges}

Many guidelines recommend a target estradiol range of roughly **100-200 pg/mL** for adult transfeminine GAHT. Short periods in the follicular range above about **30 pg/mL** may be tolerable, but long-term low estrogen and low androgen levels should be avoided because of bone and menopausal-symptom risks.

Key labs are usually **estradiol and testosterone**. Prolactin is worth following, especially when using cyproterone acetate or when levels are elevated. LH and FSH are mainly useful during puberty suppression or for assessing gonadal suppression. Progesterone usually has limited monitoring value.

## Treatment Components {#therapy}

The goal of feminizing hormone therapy is to bring estradiol and testosterone into typical adult female ranges. This is usually done with estradiol alone or estradiol combined with an antiandrogen.

### Estrogens {#estrogens}

Clinical guidance generally recommends **estradiol** as the estrogen source for GAHT. Common routes include oral tablets, sublingual or buccal use of oral tablets, transdermal patches, transdermal gel, and injections.

| Route | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|-|-|-|
| Oral | Lower cost, convenient, often easier to obtain | Lower bioavailability at the same dose; higher venous thrombosis risk |
| Sublingual/buccal | Uses oral tablets; higher bioavailability; lower thrombosis risk than swallowed oral use | Absorption varies with saliva, food, water, and talking; shorter duration |
| Patch | Convenient; lowest thrombosis risk; stable delivery | Higher cost; may be harder to obtain; skin reactions possible |
| Gel | Convenient and flexible; low thrombosis risk; good bioavailability | Skin reactions possible; affected by clothing friction, bathing, or swimming |
| Injection | Can support estradiol monotherapy; high estradiol can suppress testosterone; long interval | Injection pain, site reactions, technique-dependent variation, harder access |

Synthetic or non-estradiol estrogens such as **ethinylestradiol** and **conjugated estrogens** are not recommended for GAHT because their thrombotic risk is higher than natural estradiol. Estrone and estriol are weaker natural estrogens and are not supported by current guidance as substitutes for estradiol in GAHT.

### Progestogens {#progestogens}

Progestogen use in transfeminine GAHT remains controversial and is not well studied. Some recent data suggest progesterone or medroxyprogesterone acetate may improve satisfaction with breast development or mental state, but evidence is not enough to confirm improved breast-development outcomes. Unless an antiandrogenic effect is specifically needed, progestogens are usually not necessary.

### Antiandrogens {#antiandrogens}

For transfeminine people with gonads, estradiol is often combined with an antiandrogen. After orchiectomy or genital surgery that removes the testes, antiandrogens are usually no longer needed.

Common options include cyproterone acetate, medroxyprogesterone acetate, GnRH analogues, high-dose estradiol monotherapy, spironolactone, and bicalutamide. Bicalutamide is not recommended for routine GAHT because transfeminine evidence is limited and liver toxicity is a concern. Finasteride and dutasteride have limited evidence for routine use when testosterone is already adequately suppressed.

### Puberty Blockers {#puberty-blockers}

For adolescents in puberty, puberty suppression with GnRH analogues may be considered under specialist care. This can delay or pause puberty, suppress gonadal function, and provide time before starting gender-affirming hormone therapy.

## Monitoring {#monitoring}

Regular monitoring is important for both effectiveness and safety. See [Monitoring During Treatment]({{< ref "monitoring" >}}) for more detail.

- Check baseline health before starting hormone therapy.
- During the first year, check hormones about every three months or as directed by a clinician.
- Once stable, check hormones and routine safety labs at least yearly.
- Consider monitoring complete blood count, electrolytes, liver and kidney function, adverse effects, and medication interactions.

## Risk Overview {#risks}

See [Medication Risks]({{< ref "risk" >}}) for more detail.

The most common estrogen adverse effects include breast tenderness or pain, abdominal pain, edema, and headache. The most serious risks include venous thromboembolism, myocardial infarction, stroke, and breast cancer. Oral estradiol and synthetic estrogens carry higher thrombotic risk than transdermal estradiol.

Cyproterone acetate is associated with weight change, depression, reduced libido, erectile dysfunction, reduced sperm production, liver toxicity, liver tumors, meningioma, and thromboembolism. Bicalutamide is associated with liver toxicity and rare pulmonary toxicity. Spironolactone can cause hyperkalemia and gastrointestinal symptoms.

{{< alert theme="danger" >}}
If you notice any health problem during treatment, seek medical care immediately.
{{< /alert >}}

## Other Precautions {#precautions}

- Use the lowest dose that maintains the desired effect.
- Increase doses gradually and taper when stopping where clinically appropriate.
- Hormone therapy can impair fertility. If fertility preservation is desired, consider it before starting HRT.
- Avoid long-term states where both estradiol and testosterone are low, as this can cause bone loss and menopausal symptoms.
- Do not smoke during hormone therapy; drink alcohol cautiously; exercise appropriately.
- Consult clinicians and read drug labels before combining GAHT with other medications.

## Non-Binary Options {#non-binary}

Some non-binary people may want estrogenic bone protection or partial feminization while avoiding pronounced breast development. Selective estrogen receptor modulators (SERMs), such as raloxifene, are sometimes discussed as possible alternatives, but require medical supervision and individualized risk assessment.
