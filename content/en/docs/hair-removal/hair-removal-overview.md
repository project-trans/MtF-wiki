## 1. Hair Growth and Hair-Removal Principles

### Anatomy

First, understand the **follicular unit**: a **cluster of hair follicles** sharing one opening and accessory structures such as the sebaceous gland and arrector pili muscle. The number varies by site; beard, axillary, and pubic areas more often contain multiple hairs.

**Segments** from superficial to deep:

- **Pore**: from the follicular opening to the sebaceous-gland opening.
- **Isthmus**: from below the sebaceous-gland opening to the arrector pili insertion; the outer root sheath here is closely related to the **bulge region**.
- **Bulge region**: the main reservoir of hair-follicle stem cells and key to regrowth. The **bulge region** lies on the peripheral wall of the follicle, not in the central hair-shaft canal.
- **Upper follicle**: above the bulb; a transition area where matrix cells differentiate upward and keratinize.
- **Bulb**: the deepest end, containing **matrix cells** and **melanocytes**. The **dermal papilla** sits beneath it and provides signals and nutrition.

### Growth Cycle

Hair growth follows a cycle. Cycles vary by body site, but mainly include:

- **Anagen**: matrix cells proliferate actively, pigment activity is high, and the follicle is deep and “wet.” **This is when device-based treatment is easiest to hit.**
- **Catagen**: rapid apoptotic regression occurs; the bulb moves upward and pigment decreases.
- **Telogen**[^21]: a **club hair** forms, the follicle is shallower with low metabolism, and the hair then **sheds**.

Only a limited proportion of follicles are in the ideal phase, anagen, at the same time. Sibling follicles within a follicular unit enter anagen when local competing signals are released and their own dermal papillae activate. This is why permanent overall hair reduction or permanent hair removal requires **multiple passes over time**.

### How Different Hair-Removal Methods Act on Deeper Structures

#### Terminology

- **Permanent hair reduction**: a **long-term, stable decrease in the number of regrowing hairs** observed at **6/9/12 months** after completing a treatment course. It is **not the same as** “all hair never grows back”[^1] [^2].
- **Permanent hair removal**: specifically refers to electrolysis.

---

#### A. Laser/Intense Pulsed Light (Laser/IPL)

> A common way to achieve **permanent hair reduction**.

- Melanin absorbs laser light at specific wavelengths and converts it to heat. Heat diffuses from the bulb toward the bulge region, causing **irreversible cellular injury** and inhibiting regrowth[^5].
- **Phase sensitivity**: **Anagen hairs** contain more melanin, have deeper bulbs, and conduct heat more readily to key structures. This is the **best timing**.
- **Limits**: **Pigment dependence** and **thermal diffusion distance** limit penetration and damage to fine/light hairs or deep targets.

---

#### B. Electrolysis

> Works for **any hair color** and is the path to **permanent hair removal**.

Types:

- **Thermolysis (RF/AC)**: the electrode tip creates **cauterizing thermal coagulation**, causing **coagulative necrosis** at the follicle base, directly affecting the **dermal papilla** and potentially the **bulb**.
- **Galvanic (DC)**: electrolysis generates sodium hydroxide, affecting the **dermal papilla** and nearby stem-cell niche.
- **Blend**: RF raises temperature and ion mobility, **enhancing DC alkali production** and synergizing with thermal injury.

**Phase sensitivity**: **Anagen hairs** are preferable because follicles are deeper and tissue water content helps concentrate energy and/or ionic products near the papilla.

---

#### C. Plucking/Waxing

- Hair shafts are removed by **mechanical traction**. In **anagen**, the bulb and outer root sheath adhere tightly; **telogen** hairs are **club hairs** and are more easily pulled out whole[^6].
- **Adverse-reaction risks**: traction and shear can cause micro-injury at follicular openings and **ingrown hairs/bacterial folliculitis**[^7] [^8] [^9]. Rarely, **Pseudomonas folliculitis outbreaks** or severe perineal/vulvar **infections** may occur[^11] [^12] [^13].
- **Interaction with later energy-based therapy**: plucking/waxing removes the **melanin-containing target structure** and disrupts the hair cycle, reducing **laser/IPL** coupling efficiency. Authoritative organizations generally recommend avoiding plucking/waxing for **at least 4-6 weeks before treatment** and using **shaving** instead during treatment[^14] [^15].
- **Risk mechanism**: animal models suggest **high-density plucking** may induce synchronized regeneration of surrounding follicles[^16].

#### D. Chemical Depilatories

- Chemical depilatories break keratin disulfide bonds and **dissolve/soften** hair exposed in the upper pore. **Deep structures are not damaged**.
- They can irritate the **stratum corneum** and temporarily disrupt the barrier, but **do not affect the dermal papilla or bulb** and only **temporarily remove exposed hair shafts**.

#### E. Surface Cutting

- **Mechanical cutting** of the hair shaft at the skin surface; it **does not touch** the follicular organ. The illusion of coarser hair comes from the optical/tactile effect of the **blunt cut end**, not thicker growth.

## 3. Energy/Optical Hair-Removal Treatment Guide

Because medical electrolysis is fairly limited in mainland China, this chapter provides practical guidance for energy/optical hair removal.

### Expected Effects and Treatment Course

{{< alert theme="warning" >}}
The following data are for general people receiving **medical-grade laser hair removal** who are **not on HRT**. For transgender women, body hair often gradually becomes finer and slower-growing after estrogen/antiandrogen therapy, so fewer sessions may be needed to achieve **permanent hair reduction**.
{{< /alert >}}

#### Follow-Up Intervals by Body Site

> Overall timing can refer to [Mayo Clinic](https://www.mayoclinic.org/tests-procedures/laser-hair-removal/about/pac-20394555) (upper lip **4-8 weeks**, back **12-16 weeks**) and [American Academy of Dermatology (AAD)](https://www.aad.org/public/cosmetic/hair-removal/laser-hair-removal-faqs) (most people **4-6 weeks**). [ASLMS](https://www.aslms.org/for-the-public/treatments-using-lasers-and-energy-based-devices/laser-hair-removal) explicitly notes that clinical practice often uses a **4-8 week** rhythm and is **effect-driven**, rather than scheduled strictly by body site or a supposed “exact window.”

- Upper lip/chin: **every 4-8 weeks**[^17]
- Axilla/bikini: **every 4-6 weeks**[^18]
- Forearm/lower leg: **every 8-10 weeks**[^19]
- Back/trunk: **every 12-16 weeks**[^20]

#### Initial Course and Expectations Under Ideal Settings

Assuming suitable wavelength/pulse width/spot size and cooling, adequate coverage, and good adherence:

- **Most areas**: start with **2-6 sessions**; the first treatment usually reduces hair by 10-25% ([AAD](https://www.aad.org/public/cosmetic/hair-removal/laser-hair-removal-faqs "Laser hair removal: FAQs")).
- **Face/hormone-sensitive areas**: **often require more sessions (commonly ≥ 6)** and may need maintenance. **Some individuals** may reach **9 sessions** (see [Cleveland Clinic](https://health.clevelandclinic.org/10-things-know-laser-hair-removal "Laser Hair Removal: Is It Worth It?"), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7190465/ "Laser Treatment in Hirsutism: An Update - PMC")).

### Choosing the Right Device

{{< alert theme="success" >}}
**How should IPL, 755 nm Alexandrite, 810 nm Diode laser, and 1064 nm Nd:YAG be chosen?**
{{< /alert >}}

**People with darker skin, larger follicles, more pigment, and deeper follicle locations are better suited to longer-wavelength laser hair removal.**

Even if a hospital offers IPL for hair removal, laser hair removal is recommended. IPL is more divergent and its energy density is more dispersed than laser. Laser hair removal can precisely set pulse width (10-100 ms) to match the follicle. Because IPL has a broad spectrum, even adjustable pulse width cannot precisely match every wavelength to the follicle and may overheat the epidermis.

Based on OMLC spectra, the three wavelengths can be summarized as follows[^26]:

- 755 nm: strong melanin absorption, low Hb, low water; the most “melanin-selective.”
- 810 nm: moderate melanin, low Hb, low water; the global mainstream balance of “efficiency x safety.”
- 1064 nm: low melanin, low Hb, rising water absorption; deeper penetration and safer for darker skin, but the lowest “hit rate” on melanin, requiring compensation through higher energy, longer pulse width, or similar measures.

{{< alert theme="info" >}}
For MtF people, mandibular hair follicles may be as deep as those in cisgender men. HRT may shrink follicles but does not make their position shallower, so if 810 nm is not effective enough, choosing the more penetrating 1064 nm laser is reasonable. \
Laser hair removal can destroy germinative follicle cells and the dermal papilla, potentially causing permanent follicle loss and producing a fully smooth mandibular area.
{{< /alert >}}

### Contraindications

These mainly involve skin health, systemic diseases, and medications such as photosensitizing drugs (tetracyclines, retinoids, some anticoagulants). Tell your doctor about your personal circumstances when seeking care.

### Preoperative Preparation Consensus

- **Avoid plucking/waxing/electrolysis**: stop at least **4-6 weeks** beforehand (see [Mayo Clinic](https://www.mayoclinic.org/tests-procedures/laser-hair-removal/about/pac-20394555), [StatPearls](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK507861/)).
- **Shave about 24 hours before treatment**: leave **slight stubble** before treatment and avoid exposed long hair. **Avoid shaving immediately before treatment**, as this can increase epidermal irritation/erythema. Shaving is generally **not recommended < 6-12 hours before** or **> 48-72 hours before** treatment (see [StatPearls](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK507861/), [Mayo Clinic](https://www.mayoclinic.org/tests-procedures/laser-hair-removal/about/pac-20394555)).

### Postoperative Care Consensus

**Pigment changes** and **blistering/burning sensation** are the main complications[^22]. In this situation, **avoid sun exposure** and **cool adequately**[^23].

Use icing + daily sun protection[^24] + avoid heat/friction (≥ 48 h)[^25] + gentle skin care + shave only, do not pluck. Watch for abnormalities and seek care promptly if needed.

## 4. Common Misconceptions QA

### Can laser hair removal make an area hairless?

No. It can achieve long-term hair reduction; see [permanent hair reduction](#terminology).

### Are the hair-growth-suppressing claims of waxing and some depilatory creams true?

By mechanism, their primary effect is temporary removal of existing hair. They do not provide long-term suppression of hair growth.

### Is a longer laser hair-removal wavelength always better?

Longer wavelengths penetrate deeper, but melanin absorption decreases. Choose the [appropriate wavelength](#choosing-the-right-device) based on skin color, follicle depth, and amount of melanin.

### Do home hair-removal devices really work?

Most home devices are IPL. They have lower energy and worse melanin selectivity than hospital devices, so effects are limited even when present. Class III medical devices used for laser hair removal are regulated and can only be used in hospitals.

### Is more frequent or more numerous laser hair removal always better?

Treatment should roughly follow the [hair growth cycle](#expected-effects-and-treatment-course).
